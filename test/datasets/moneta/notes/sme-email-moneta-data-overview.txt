Hi folks,

Sorry this is a bit of a wall of text — I figured it was easier to write it all down once than answer the same questions in Slack for the next two weeks. I’ve been living in this Moneta dataset for a while (retail banking + a bit of brokerage on the side), so here’s how I think about it day to day.

At the center of everything is the clients list. That’s our customers — people we onboarded into the bank. Each person has a client_id that we use everywhere else; that id is always filled in, and no two people share the same one. We keep their first_name and last_name, an email (should be unique when present — we use it for statements and password resets), a phone number, and when they were registered (created_at). Address-wise we store country, city, and address (street-level). There’s also a segment field that tells us how we service them — in practice it’s one of REGULAR, WEALTH, or ULTRA. If someone is missing a name or email it’s annoying for ops, but the one thing that absolutely cannot be blank is the client id.

Every client can have one or more accounts. You always join accounts back to clients on client_id — an account never floats around without an owner, and one person can easily have several accounts. Each account has its own account_id (always present, never duplicated), an account_type which is basically checking or savings, a balance (current money sitting there — this should never go negative in our books; if you see a negative balance something’s wrong), and opened_at for when the account was opened. Balance is the live snapshot, not a history.

Money movement lives in transactions. Each row is one movement on an account — you always go through account_id to get there (transactions don’t point at the client directly; you go account → client if you need the person). Types we care about are deposit, withdrawal, and transfer (transaction_type). The amount is how much moved; it should always be greater than zero — we don’t store signed amounts, the type tells you the direction. There’s a transaction_date and a free-text description (memo-ish, often messy, don’t overthink it). Every transaction needs an id (transaction_id) and must belong to a real account.

On the lending side we have loans. Loans hang off the client directly (client_id) — not off an account. So a person can have loans even if you’re looking at a different account of theirs. Each loan has loan_id, the principal amount (should be positive), an interest_rate (annual; also shouldn’t be negative or absurdly huge — if you see rates in the hundreds that’s bad data, treat with suspicion), start_date and end_date, and a status. Status in the wild is active, closed, or defaulted. One thing that bites people: end date should be on or after start date. I’ve seen rows where end is before start — those are garbage, don’t use them for tenure calculations without cleaning.

Repayments are in loan_payments. Each payment belongs to exactly one loan via loan_id (you don’t attach payments to the client directly). Fields are payment_id, payment_date, and amount paid that day. Amounts should be positive. Payment id is always there.

Then there’s the investment / brokerage half of the house.

stock_portfolios are investment “buckets” owned by a client (client_id again). A client can have several portfolios. Each has a portfolio_id, a portfolio_name (often looks like PORTF-XXXXX — not super friendly, but that’s what we have), and created_at for when the portfolio was set up. Portfolio id is required and unique.

The catalog of instruments is stocks — this is reference data, not “who owns what.” Each stock has stock_id, a ticker (should be unique — we don’t want two rows for the same ticker), company_name, sector (things like Energy, Technology, Healthcare, Retail), and exchange (NASDAQ, NYSE, LSE, HKEX, etc.). Tickers and stock ids shouldn’t be blank.

What a client actually owns sits in stock_holdings. A holding always belongs to a portfolio (portfolio_id) and points at a stock (stock_id). So to answer “what does this person hold?” you go client → portfolios → holdings → stocks. Holdings have shares (number of shares — should be > 0; a zero-share holding is noise) and average_price (average cost per share — also should be positive). holding_id identifies the row.

Orders are separate from holdings. trade_orders are buy/sell instructions placed against a portfolio for a given stock — again portfolio_id + stock_id. order_type is buy or sell, order_date is when it was placed, shares is how many they wanted, limit_price is the limit they set (should be positive when present), and status is pending, executed, or cancelled. Important: an order is not the same as a holding. Executed buys should show up in holdings eventually, but the tables are separate and we don’t always have perfect timing between them. Order id is always filled in.

How I usually explain the “shape” to new people:

- Clients are the people.
- Accounts and loans and portfolios all hang off the client.
- Transactions hang off accounts (not clients).
- Loan payments hang off loans.
- Holdings and trade orders hang off portfolios, and both also point at stocks.
- Stocks stand alone as the instrument list.

A few practical gotchas from support tickets:

- Don’t join transactions straight to clients — go through accounts.
- Don’t assume every client has a loan or a portfolio; plenty only have a checking account.
- Loan status wording is lending-specific (active / closed / defaulted). Trade order status is different (pending / executed / cancelled). Same column name status, different meanings — please don’t mix them up in reports.
- Amounts on transactions, loans, payments, holdings, and orders should all be positive numbers. If something is zero or negative, flag it.
- Ids (client_id, account_id, transaction_id, loan_id, payment_id, portfolio_id, stock_id, holding_id, order_id) are always present in good rows; blanks there mean the extract is broken.
- Client emails and stock tickers are the main “business unique” fields people care about besides the ids.
- Dates are stored as text-ish date strings (yyyy-mm-dd style). Loan start/end ordering is the one date rule that actually matters for analytics.

If you’re building anything customer-facing, start from clients and walk outward. If you’re doing cash-flow analysis, accounts + transactions. Credit risk is loans + payments. Brokerage is portfolios + holdings + orders, with stocks as the lookup for ticker/sector/exchange.

Happy to jump on a call if something’s unclear — I know this is a lot. Just wanted you to have the “tribal knowledge” in one place before you start wiring reports.
